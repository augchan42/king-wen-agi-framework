## Problem

The King Wen AGI Framework paper (v0.1, Zenodo DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.14679538) had structural issues and lacked the depth needed to support its hypothesis:

- Duplicate abstract, broken section numbering, duplicate keywords section
- ~25% of paper body was inline Python code
- No Related Work section
- No baseline comparisons (the obvious criticism: "how do you know this isn't just any non-random ordering?")
- Thin 2-sentence conclusion
- No concrete validation methodology
- Hedging language ("may represent", "potentially") weakened a testable claim
- No connection to the OpenSpiel-based simulation being developed in warringstates-day

## Goal

Revise the paper to arXiv preprint quality with a strong hypothesis, proper Related Work, statistical baselines, and a concrete OpenSpiel validation design. Framing: **option B (stronger hypothesis)** — "The King Wen sequence implements a curriculum learning optimization."

---

## Design Decisions

### Audience and Venue

**arXiv preprint** — position paper with simulation proposal. Not targeting a specific conference or journal yet. The paper stakes the claim and methodology; a follow-up paper will report simulation results.

### Core Claim

**Stronger hypothesis (not hedging):** The King Wen sequence implements a curriculum learning optimization. The evidence is mathematical and empirical; the validation is proposed (OpenSpiel simulation).

Rationale: The user has a concrete toolchain (OpenSpiel), concrete data (chinese-classics-reference corpus), a concrete test subject (Han as worst-case), and concrete metrics. Hedging undersells what the math already shows and makes the results paper look post-hoc.

### Naming Conventions

- **I-Ching** (hyphenated) throughout body text. Book titles retain original form (e.g., "The I Ching: A Biography").
- **BC/AD** for all dates. Never BCE/CE.

### What's Not In This Paper

- The narrative engine and image generation pipeline (separate concern, separate paper)
- The warringstates.day website architecture
- Jiaoshi Yilin analysis (mentioned as future work only)

### 2. Related Work (3 subsections, ~1.5 pages)

**2.1 Curriculum Learning**
- Bengio et al. (2009) — foundational paper
- Graves et al. (2017) — automated curriculum learning
- Wang et al. (2022) — survey establishing taxonomy (difficulty measurer + training scheduler)
- Position: King Wen as pre-computational curriculum design

**2.2 I-Ching in Computational Intelligence**
- Chen et al. (2016) — IDEA evolutionary algorithm
- Zhang et al. (2021) — AS-NAS neural architecture search
- Petoukhov (2017) — biological coding parallels
- Key distinction: prior work uses I-Ching *transformation mechanics* as algorithmic primitives; we analyze the *sequence ordering itself*

**2.3 Information-Theoretic Surprise and Optimal Learning**
- Schmidhuber (2006) — artificial curiosity, intermediate surprise
- Itti & Baldi (2009) — Bayesian surprise as KL divergence
- Nielsen (2020) — information geometry framework
- Position: King Wen surprise profile matches the optimal intermediate-surprise prescription

### 3. Key Observations
- Largely unchanged from v1

### 5. Empirical Analysis (promoted to own section)

**Baselines:**
1. Random orderings — 1,000 permutations, mean + 95% CI (null hypothesis)
2. Natural binary ordering (0-63)
3. Shao Yong ordering (c. 1050 AD) — most important baseline

**Metrics per ordering (63 transitions):**
- Hamming distance distribution
- Pattern similarity distribution
- Information-theoretic surprise distribution
- Variance of surprise
- Autocorrelation of surprise at lags 1-10

**Statistical tests:**
- Kolmogorov-Smirnov: distribution difference
- Levene's: variance difference
- Ljung-Box: autocorrelation significance

**New figure needed:** 4-panel comparison (King Wen, random mean, binary, Shao Yong) — to be generated by extending `generate_plots.py`

### 6. Relationship to Modern Learning Theory
- Tightened prose with inline citations
- Subsections: Curriculum Learning, Gradient Descent Optimization, Meta-Learning, Developmental Learning

### 7. Implications
- Concrete implications with bold labels
- Learning rate schedules, local minima escape, multi-dimensional curricula, surprise budgeting

### 8. Proposed Validation: OpenSpiel Simulation

**Game formulation:**
- 7-player extensive-form game, custom OpenSpiel environment
- State space: territory, military, alliances, resources
- Action space: military moves, diplomatic proposals, internal policy
- Turn structure: simultaneous diplomatic + sequential military

**Experimental design:**
- Control: all 7 states use standard RL (CFR or PPO)
- Test: Han uses King Wen-informed learning; other 6 use standard RL
- Han chosen as maximum-difficulty case (smallest, poorest, first to fall historically)

**Agent architecture:**
- Control: CFR or PPO (OpenSpiel built-in)
- King Wen Han: same base algorithm, learning rate + exploration governed by King Wen sequence
- Oracle interface: hash of game state features modulo 64 → King Wen position → curriculum stage → modulates learning rate and exploration
- Future alternatives: Meihua Yishu, Jiaoshi Yilin (mentioned, not implemented)

**Metrics (50-100 runs per config):**
- Survival duration
- Territory held over time (AUC)
- Alliance stability and count
- Final ranking

**Statistical analysis:**
- Mann-Whitney U test per metric
- Cohen's d effect size
- Learning curves (metric vs. run number)

**Open questions:**
- Oracle interface richness (hash-modulo vs. Meihua vs. Yilin)
- Weighting robustness (α₁, α₂, α₃ sensitivity)
- Learning speed vs. decision quality
- Generalization to other states (Yan, Chu)

### 9. Conclusion
- 3 substantive paragraphs
- Summarizes findings, claims curriculum encoding, references baselines
- Points to OpenSpiel as the concrete test
- Notes repo availability
